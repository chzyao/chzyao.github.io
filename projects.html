Two-Scale Neural Networks for Partial Differential Equations with Small Parameters

Qiao Zhuang, Chris Ziyi Yao, Zhongqiang Zhang, and George Em Karniadakis Communications in Computational Physics 2025

We propose a two-scale neural network method for solving partial differential equations (PDEs) with small parameters using physics-informed neural networks (PINNs). We directly incorporate the small parameters into the architecture of neural networks. The proposed method enables solving PDEs with small parameters in a simple fashion, without adding Fourier features or other computationally taxing searches of truncation parameters. Various numerical examples demonstrate reasonable accuracy in capturing features of large derivatives in the solutions caused by small parameters.

Foldable Aeroshell Demonstrator

Department of Aeronautics, Imperial College London May 2023 - June 2023

The Foldable Aeroshell Demonstrator builds on Imperial College’s HATHOR (“Hypersonic foldable Aeroshell for Thermal protection using Origami”) research programme. While traditional rigid heat shields like Soyuz and Starliner are limited by launcher fairing size, foldable aeroshells can be stowed during launch and deployed before atmospheric entry, enabling heavier payload delivery. Our group designed a miniaturised, free-flight demonstrator manufacturable and testable within a £150 k budget.

I was responsible for the design of the Command and Data Handling (C&DH) and Telemetry, Tracking & Command (TT&C) subsystems. My work focused on defining the onboard communication architecture, performing data-budget analysis, and implementing ground-station communication modelling in Simulink. I selected and integrated the SpaceWire bus and a real-time operating system with LEON processors to support reliable data transfer between payload and telemetry units. I also developed the telemetry packet structure, simulated transmission reliability under noise and delay, and validated communication performance across mission phases.

Conceptual Design of a Reginal Airliner Jet

Department of Aeronautics, Imperial College London October 2022 - December 2022

Our group designed a 90-passenger regional jet for multi-leg domestic routes in New Zealand, developed in accordance with FAR 25 requirements. The project involved complete conceptual sizing of the aircraft — including aerodynamics, propulsion integration, systems layout, and performance evaluation.

I was responsible for the fuselage design, undercarriage configuration, and aerodynamic drag estimation. This included cabin layout optimisation for safety and comfort standards, geometric sizing of the empennage and cockpit, positioning and sizing of landing gear to ensure clearance and stability, and calculation of component drag contributions using the component build-up method.

Preliminary and Detailed Design of a Regional Airliner Jet

Department of Aeronautics, Imperial College London January 2023 - March 2023

This project followed the conceptual design of a 90-passenger regional jet and focused on the structural design and analysis of its primary airframe components — wings, fuselage, and empennage — under multiple load cases, including symmetric flight, One-engine-inoperative (OEI), and nose-off landing. Our team developed analytical models for shear, bending, and torsion, optimised component weight and stiffness, and evaluated composite versus metallic configurations for manufacturability and fatigue resistance.

I was responsible for the fuselage structural design, including the sizing of skin, stringers, and frames under pressurisation and bending loads. My work involved deriving load distributions, performing buckling and stability checks using analytical and numerical methods, and conducting material selection and optimisation through CES EduPack (now known as Ansys Granta EduPack) and MATLAB.
